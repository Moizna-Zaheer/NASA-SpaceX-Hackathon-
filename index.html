Melting Polar Ice

Rising global temperatures due to increased GHG emissions cause polar ice to melt. This leads to sea level rise, threatening coastal communities and ecosystems. Melting ice also reduces the Earth’s albedo, causing further warming. The loss of ice habitats affects polar species, such as polar bears and seals.

The Carbon Cycle

The carbon cycle is the natural process of carbon exchange among the atmosphere, oceans, and living organisms. Human activities, such as burning fossil fuels and deforestation, disrupt this cycle by increasing atmospheric CO₂ levels.

This disruption enhances the greenhouse effect, leading to global warming. Oceans absorb excess CO₂, causing ocean acidification, which affects marine life.

Cutting Carbon: The Path to a Cleaner Future.

Greenhouse gas emissions from activities like burning fossil fuels and deforestation are key drivers of climate change.

Reducing emissions through renewable energy, sustainable practices, and efficient technologies is vital to slowing global warming and protecting our planet.

Frequently asked questions

  • 1. What are Greenhouse Gases (GHGs) and why are they important?

    Greenhouse gases (GHGs) are gases in the atmosphere, such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O), that trap heat and contribute to the greenhouse effect. They are important because they regulate Earth’s temperature. However, excess GHG emissions from human activities are causing global warming and climate change.

  • 2. What are the main sources of greenhouse gas emissions?

    The main sources of GHG emissions include:

    • Burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas) for electricity and transportation
    • Industrial activities like cement and steel production
    • Waste management, including methane emissions from landfills.
    • Agriculture, especially livestock and rice production
  • 3. How do fossil fuels contribute to greenhouse gas emissions?

    Fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas) release large amounts of carbon dioxide (CO₂) when burned for energy production, transportation, and industry. This CO₂ is the largest contributor to GHG emissions, accelerating global warming and environmental damage.

Deforestation releases carbon stored in trees back into the atmosphere as CO₂ when trees are cut down or burned. Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO₂. Destroying them not only reduces this absorption capacity but also releases significant amounts of GHGs, contributing to climate change.

Agriculture contributes to GHGs primarily through:
  • Methane from livestock digestion (enteric fermentation) and manure
  • Nitrous oxide from fertilizers and soil management
  • Rice paddies, which produce methane during cultivation.

Industrial processes, such as cement, steel, and chemical production, emit significant CO₂ and other greenhouse gases. Factories burn fossil fuels for energy and release gases like methane and fluorinated gases during chemical reactions, which contribute to global warming.

The carbon cycle is the natural process by which carbon is exchanged between the atmosphere, oceans, soil, and living organisms. Human activities, particularly burning fossil fuels and deforestation, are adding excess carbon to the atmosphere, disrupting the cycle and leading to higher CO₂ levels, which drive global warming.

Methane (CH₄) is a potent greenhouse gas, with a warming potential much higher than CO₂. It is produced in landfills when organic waste decomposes anaerobically and from livestock (especially cows) in agriculture. Methane emissions are a significant contributor to the enhanced greenhouse effect and climate change.
